How did people learn it?
Tomorrow's
Warriors
Talent development
infrastructure

Founded in 1991 by bassist Gary Crosby, Tomorrow's Warriors was a jazz education programme built for young Black and minority ethnic musicians excluded from conventional pathways. It met in community centres. It was cheap or free. It produced Moses Boyd, Nubya Garcia, and Shabaka Hutchings — the names on those festival line-ups.

↳ Without Tomorrow's Warriors, the personnel of the UK jazz renaissance is substantially different.

Gilles Peterson — broadcaster

"Gary Crosby created an alternative pathway. Tomorrow's Warriors was doing what the system wasn't — giving these kids a musical home."

Who guided them?
The Jazz
Warriors
Mentorship
infrastructure

The Jazz Warriors were a large ensemble founded in 1986 — Courtney Pine, Cleveland Watkiss, Orphy Robinson — who played explicitly political jazz during the anti-apartheid era. They did not reach the mainstream. They stayed, taught, and mentored the generation that did. Their relative invisibility in the official story of British music is itself part of the argument.

↳ Courtney Pine appeared on the cover of The Wire in 1986. By the 1990s, mainstream coverage had largely moved on.

Gilles Peterson — broadcaster

"That whole generation kept the flame going in a way that didn't get recognised. Courtney never stopped working."

Where did they meet?
TRC &
Steam Down
Physical & community
infrastructure

Total Refreshment Centre in Dalston was a cheap rehearsal space, venue, and studio. Steam Down was a weekly Wednesday session in Deptford — part gig, part jam, part congregation. Affordable and open because they belonged to the scene, not to an institution. They created conditions for collaboration no conservatoire could replicate.

↳ Steam Down ran weekly for years before any of its participants received mainstream press coverage.

Gilles Peterson — broadcaster

"Something was being created in real time, every week. Those spaces mattered enormously."

Three structures. Three answers to the same question: how did this music exist before anyone powerful was paying attention?

Stage 3 — What changed?

The institutions arrive

For decades, the structures you have just read about operated without mainstream recognition. The music they produced was not considered important by critics, major labels, national broadcasters, or prize committees.

Then the same music — made by the same communities, through the same infrastructure — began to be celebrated by exactly the institutions that had previously ignored it.

2019
Ezra Collective sign to Partisan Records and release You Can't Steal My Joy — the album title taken directly from the experience of cultural marginalisation.
2023
Ezra Collective win the Mercury Prize for Where I'm Meant to Be. The album also charts at number 1. Jazz on the main stage.
2021
Nubya Garcia performs at Glastonbury and is profiled in the Guardian, The Times, and Vogue. Jazz appears on mainstream festival line-ups as a headline draw.
Ongoing
Major labels, arts funding bodies, and national broadcasters begin actively pursuing artists who built careers entirely outside those structures.

Is that reclamation? Validation? Something else?

The neat story — exclusion, then recognition — breaks down here. The music did not change to earn mainstream approval. The mainstream changed its position. Whether that represents justice, co-option, or something more complicated is not a question with a clean answer.
